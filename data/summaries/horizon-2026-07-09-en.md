## [IERS: No Leap Second at End of December 2026](https://datacenter.iers.org/data/latestVersion/bulletinC.txt) ⭐️ 8.0/10

The International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service (IERS) has announced in its latest bulletin that no leap second will be introduced at the end of December 2026, leaving the UTC-TAI offset unchanged. This decision maintains the status quo for systems dependent on precise timekeeping, avoiding the disruptions that leap second insertions often cause in computing, network synchronization, and critical infrastructure. The UTC-TAI offset has been -37 seconds since January 1, 2017, and no leap seconds have been added since then. The announcement also notes that the UTC-GPS offset remains at -18 seconds, as GPS time has a constant 19-second offset from TAI.

hackernews · ChrisArchitect · Jul 9, 14:16 · [Discussion](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48846281)

**Background**: Leap seconds are periodic one-second adjustments to Coordinated Universal Time (UTC) to keep it synchronized with Earth’s slightly irregular rotation. Since 1972, 27 leap seconds have been added. The IERS, which monitors Earth’s orientation, decides when to insert them. The last leap second was added at the end of 2016, and since then, no further adjustments have been needed.

Key details: DNS-01 challenge proves domain ownership via a DNS TXT record, enabling certificates without public HTTP exposure; wildcard certificates can prevent hostname leakage in Certificate Transparency logs; split-horizon DNS requires maintaining separate DNS views, which commenters argued creates unnecessary overhead.

Internal services often require TLS to encrypt traffic, but managing internal certificate authorities can be cumbersome due to trust store configuration.
